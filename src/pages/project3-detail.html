TAFLab Ocean Drones Project

Overview

I'm developing embedded firmware for autonomous sailboat drones at UC Berkeley's TAFLab as part of the CAPTAIn project. Working under Dr. Reza Alam with PhD mentor Arsh Anis Khan, I built the complete STM32-based control system from scratch and conduct live testing at Berkeley Marina. My work enables these boats to autonomously collect oceanographic data for months without human intervention.

What I Built

  • Complete Firmware Architecture: Developed entire embedded system using STM32H723ZGT6 microcontroller
  • Custom Sensor Drivers: Built HAL-based drivers for GPS, IMU, wind sensors with interrupt-driven architecture
  • PWM Control Systems: Programmed precise servo control for sail and rudder positioning
  • Communication Protocols: Developed satellite and radio systems for real-time data transmission

Technologies Used

  • STM32H723ZGT6 with LQFP144 MPU, C Programming
  • STM32 HAL Library, DMA, Interrupt-driven architecture
  • Marine-grade sensors, Satellite communication systems

Testing & Data Collection

Conducted extensive live testing at Berkeley Marina, collecting 40+ hours of operational data weekly. Validated navigation algorithms, sensor integration, and communication systems in real marine conditions. Worked in a startup-like environment developing 10 prototypes for live ocean testing.

Challenges & Learnings

Marina Rescue Missions: Became surprisingly good at kayaking out to retrieve boats that lost connection mid-test. Nothing teaches you to build better failsafe systems quite like having to paddle after your own autonomous creation before it explores San Francisco Bay solo.

Technical Challenges: Built reliable embedded systems for harsh marine environments, focusing on fault tolerance and real-time performance. The STM32 platform deepened my embedded programming skills while iterative prototype development taught me system optimization and rapid debugging techniques[5].

Why This Matters

My firmware enables autonomous sailboats to collect oceanographic data for climate research and shipping route optimization. Unlike traditional approaches, sailboats can stay out for months using wind power, position themselves precisely where needed, and operate quietly without disturbing marine life.
